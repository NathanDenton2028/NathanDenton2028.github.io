Interactive Wind Turbine Exhibit

Designed and built an interactive wind‑energy exhibit where two players assemble custom turbines and compete to generate the highest power output. The game teaches children aged 6–11 about renewable energy through hands‑on play.

In my Cornerstone of Engineering project, our four‑person team developed Turbine Trials: a portable exhibit that challenges young visitors to choose blades, motors and bases and then see whose turbine generates the most energy. We used Arduino microcontrollers to read colour‑coded button inputs and relay them to MATLAB, which calculated simulated power output and displayed scores on LCD screens. The exhibit included educational boards tailored to different age groups explaining how wind energy works and why renewable sources are important.

As team manager, I oversaw the schedule and made major contributions to both mechanical and software design. I modelled the enclosure, centre components and rotating display in SolidWorks, designed laser‑cut panels and our custom button assembly, and built the roller system and mounting brackets for the trifold display. I also constructed the mounting system for the turbines, drilled holes and installed magnets in the 3D‑printed pieces, and wired two Arduino boards to integrate all electronics. On the software side I wrote the Arduino scripts that gather player inputs and drive the LCD displays, as well as the MATLAB code that ranks players and computes energy generation. This project honed my ability to blend CAD, fabrication, electronics and programming into a cohesive user experience.

Robotic Hand Input Device

An ongoing research project to develop a wearable glove that maps human hand movements to a robotic hand using encoders and motion sensors. The device allows intuitive teleoperation of an actuated hand.

As part of Northeastern’s robotic arm project I’m leading the design of a hand input device that lets users control a robotic hand by simply moving their own. The system focuses on the knuckle and middle joints of each finger and uses miniature rotary encoders to measure flexion while ignoring lateral motion. A 9‑DOF inertial measurement unit (IMU) on the thumb captures its unique range of motion. All sensors will be integrated into a comfortable glove.

I’m currently researching existing DIY input gloves and capturing concepts from hobbyist projects to inform my design. Early SolidWorks models explore how to mount encoders on the glove without inhibiting movement, and I’m prototyping brackets that can be 3D printed or laser‑cut for quick iteration. Once the mechanical layout is finalized, microcontroller firmware will translate encoder readings into commands for the robotic hand. This project is still in development, leaving room for future improvements and additional sensing capabilities.
